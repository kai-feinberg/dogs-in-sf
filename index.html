Dogs in SF

A Ruff Idea

It was golden hour in Lafayette Park, and every mile of the sprawling 11.5-acre park was littered with dogs. Small groups of people formed in the play area, laughing and talking, while their dogs roamed around.

Russ Smith sat on a bench as his German Shepherd Husky mix, Otto, ran ecstatically around the park. Smith fit in perfectly with the scene, happily talking with friends while playing fetch with his dog. Nobody would've been able to tell from a first glance - but Otto changed his life.

Smith had lived in San Francisco for 14 years and, until recently, was working in the tech industry. He felt like his projects “were for things that didn't really improve the world.” He faced a pivotal moment in his life. “I feel like it's tough for people in my age to make new friends,” Smith said.

The Unfurtunate Reality

Smith isn't alone in feeling this way. San Francisco has the largest millennial population in the US. The city also doesn't have a large population of children, with the number of dogs living in San Francisco exceeding the number of children, according to data from the American Community Survey.

One factor contributing to this shared feeling of loneliness is the high percentage of people working from home. Compared to other major cities, like New York City, Boston and Chicago, San Francisco has nearly 15% more residents working from home.

Because of the high work-from-home rates in the city, San Franciscans lack structured workspace interactions. The pandemic caused a huge shift in everyone's lives, moving a lot of people to work from home. Technologies like Zoom and Google Meet became the norm and still persist today.

In addition, mental health issues have only been exacerbated in the past few years by the uncertainty and lack of human connection brought about by the pandemic. Social media has taken face-to-face interactions into asynchronous digital spaces.

Mental health struggles and loneliness is not a uniquely SF problem but a generational issue. But oddly enough, being in the center of the tech world and the face of the future, people are lonelier than ever. Technological innovations seem to have distanced people from each other and fostered a culture of dispassion and detachedness. But San Francisco has found a unique way to combat the challenges of the modern day: dogs.

Russ Smith's dog Otto

"I'm part of this generation where I won't ever own a home, won't get married, and won't have kids...I needed something else in my life.”
- Russ Smith

Finding Furends

Pets have become a way to overcome challenges of loneliness and disconnection. At first glance, dog culture in San Francisco may seem superficial, like a city of young people who like dogs and spoil them.

Elisa Tilley, for example, has a two-year-old dog named Millie. She explained that Millie gets bored of food easily. Tilly cooks organic foods including cooked chicken, vegetables and fruits. “I spoil her with everything: snacks, treats, anything. If we go to the store and she loves a toy I buy it for her. I mean, I spend a lot of money on my dog,” Tilley said

In San Francisco, dog-related businesses such as doggie daycares, dog walkers and dog restaurants abound. Dogue, a dog food restaurant, opened in 2015 and recently began offering a $75 tasting menu.

However, this isn't just a story about spoiled dogs. People get dogs to give them companionship but in many ways they're getting a lot more than that. Dogs have been proven to help improve mental health. They also motivate people to go outside more and interact with others. Even if people can't bring themselves to be social, they want their dogs to take walks and meet other dogs. Communities have formed in these dog parks of dog lovers.

“There's a lot of the community in these dog parks... Everyone kind of knows each other... and [I] kind of see familiar faces so it's nice that I get to go out in the community more.”
- Elisa Tilley

A Paw-some Solution

Smith adopted Otto four years ago. Even though he actively sought to get a dog, he remembers feeling unprepared for having a pet to care for. This led him to buy a bunch of tech toys, which were part of the brand Mars Petcare. Eventually, he was inspired to create activity monitors for pets and was able to test them on Otto. He went on to found Whistle, a piece of AI technology that monitors pet health. When the pandemic hit, it was also incredibly lonely for him.

Otto and Russ Smith in Lafayette Park

“[Otto] made it bearable...It was just him and I. [He] was my only reason to be outside... I think he helped a lot with just the sheer loneliness of those three years.”
- Russ Smith

Dogs have helped combat loneliness in the city, and it has extended beyond just pandemic times. In the middle of the pandemic, Nandi Hurley was out walking her dog, Zoey, a 2-year-old German Shepherd. “People are much more friendlier to dogs than humans,” Hurley said. “Everybody that I've met here around this area is because of the dog.” Josh B. was also out with his dog Kona, an Australian Shepherd. He's only lived in San Francisco for six months and works exclusively from home.

“All the people I've met so far in my six months of living in SF have all been through my dog,” said Josh.

“I spoil [my dog] with everything - snacks, treats, anything. If we go to the store and she loves a toy I buy it for her. I mean I spend a lot of money on my dog,” said Tilley.

“People are much more friendlier to dogs than humans. When I walk around people just come up to me and start conversations,” said Hurley.

The impact dogs have had on the people of San Francisco is more than just having a furry friend. And while there are many different contributing factors to this phenomenon, San Francisco is an incredibly dog-friendly city. “I would assume that a lot of people love dogs,” said Hurley. “The type of people who are San Franciscans are also dog people.”
